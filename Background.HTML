Background

The Fondom has being existing since the Precolonial 15th century.The name came from “mbu”, meaning cave. Bamumbu therefore means “People of caves”. In the early points in her origin, Bamumbu ancestors lived in caves for safety and from where they launched attacks to conquer new territories. The founder of BAMUMBU was PRINCE KETUMONGUH who left MENKA with his supporters in search of a new lands to create his own Fondom after a Royal succession dispute at the Royal Court of the Grand Widikum Tribe. Descendants of Ketumagu who had a succession dispute with his brothers in Widikum at about 15th century AD left with his two brothers Jongleku and Nkemna northward towards and settled at Tadkon (present day batibo). The next generation migrated further north and settled in besi and then Bamesong (present day Ashong), then moved slightly northwest to Gobe. In the 17th century Chamayah emerged as head of nthe family and moved further from Gobe and settled at Achuabeh (interpreted as Hill Top) which is present-day Fomenji. From 1884-1885 during the Franco-British war against the Germans for the annexation of the cameroons, chamayah and his people moved further down the hill to settle in the valley (Mbu) at Nvueh-Mezuh. Here the first palace was built thus the Bamumbu (Mbu) was established. In Nvueh- Mezuh, chamaya went for fishing and found Namambu the daughter of Megum (Folepi) who had earlier migrated from wedikum through Besi and settled at mechiebie. They got married latter and at his death had no children together. As a widow Namambu remarried his successor and they gave birth to what latter became.

“The nine sons of Namambu”.

1) Metangama
2) Fombele
3) Nyamesheke
4) Nkweta Nyonga

Most of the sons left the palace and set up their own subchiefdoms in a united Fondom under the paramount ruler of the Bamumbu People. The present palace is situated at Nchingang just about a kilometer from the site of the first palace.
GEOGRAPHICAL LOCATION
Bamumbu is a Bantu clan of the mundani tribe. The Bamumbu people speak native mundani language. The clan lies in the Mount Bamboutos Caldera, located between latitudes 5˚44' and 5˚36'N and longitudes 9˚55' and 10˚07'E, and extending from an altitude of 200 m to 2700 m above sea level, is typical of a multi-agricultural production system in the western highlands of Cameroon. It receives between 2000 - 3000 mm of rainfall annually and the mean monthly maximum and minimum temperatures are 32˚C and 17˚C, respectively. The geographical location of the clan is unique in the it is the only habitable Caldera along the Cameroon volcanic line (CVL). It occupies 2/3 of the total land surface and population of the Mundani Tribe. Alongside the Nweh Tribe they constituted two Native Authorities before independence, which were later merged after assessment to create the Nweh-Mundani Council Area. Later this changed to Fontem Sub Division, which is the present day Lebialem Division of the south west region. The Bamumbu Fondom/clan share boundaries with four major administrative divisions of Cameroon (Menoua, Bamboutos, Mezam and momo). Bamumbu equally has three vegetation zones (forest, savanna and sahelian) that favors all systems of agriculture thus making it Cameroon in miniature.
POPULATION.
BAMUMBU has an estimated population of 65000 inhabitants occupying a total land surface of about 1000 km2 with huge tourist, and agricultural potentials. A significant proportion of its population resides out of the clan/Fondom, with about 30 000 people in some parts of Littoral and West Region where they form whole villages.
AGRICULTURE
More than 98% of the local population depends on subsistence farming and some small scale of commercial agriculture by the sale of produce from cash crops. This agriculture is supplemented by petit trading, fishing, hunting and transport services dominated by bike riders most particularly during the dry season though practiced by a minute proportion of the population. The agro-pastoral activities practiced in the Bamumbu follow the bioclimatic condition of the area. Parts of the lower belt practice the cultivation of cash crops such as palm oil, cocoa, Robusta coffee, gathering of non-timber forest products, and food crops like plantains, cocoyam, cassavas bananas, pepper etc. The degree of palm oil cultivation in the lower belt is modest as compared to other parts of the tribe (Mundani). Meanwhile the extremes of the upper belt practice the cultivation of market gardening products like Irish potatoes, cabbages, carrots, garlic and licks, sweet yams, ginger etc. The intensity of market gardening in the Upper belt is very high. As well the rearing of cattle, goats, sheep and chicken etc. (Nkemteba J.T, 2002) and field survey by CEPLODEV team for WCD. The high intensity of market gardening products in the upper belt results to a higher per capital income to local farmers of this zone as compared to the lower zone where the local farmers have lower income earnings. This thus impacts the upper belt with increased economic activities by the presence of bigger markets. These increase agricultural activities generate secondary activities like transportation and commercial activities that lead to increase trade activities with the bordering Towns of Dschang, Mbouda and Bafoussam.
